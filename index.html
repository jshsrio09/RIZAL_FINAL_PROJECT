Legacy

Jose Rizal is considered the Philippines’ greatest national hero. His writings sparked the Philippine revolution and continue to inspire generations in the pursuit of truth, freedom, and justice.

Fun Fact

Rizal was a Renaissance man—he sketched, sculpted, wrote poetry, performed surgeries, and even bred pets like dogs and doves.

José Rizal, the national hero of the Philippines, was a prolific writer and intellectual whose works sparked the Filipino nationalist movement against Spanish colonial rule. His literary output includes novels, essays, poems, and articles that address themes such as colonial oppression, injustice, and the need for reform. Here are his most notable works:

Noli Me Tangere (1887)

Type: Novel

Language: Spanish

English Title: Touch Me Not

This novel exposes the social injustices, corruption, and abuse of power by the Spanish clergy and government officials in the Philippines. It follows the story of Crisostomo Ibarra, a young man who returns to the Philippines after studying in Europe, only to find his country plagued by oppression. It is a scathing critique of colonial rule and the Catholic Church's role in Philippine society.

El Filibusterismo (1891)

Type: Novel

Language: Spanish

English Title: The Reign of Greed

A darker, more radical sequel to Noli Me Tangere, this novel centers on Simoun, the alter ego of Crisostomo Ibarra. Disillusioned, Simoun returns to instigate a revolution against the Spanish regime. The novel explores themes of revenge, corruption, and social transformation, highlighting Rizal’s growing frustration with the pace of reform.

Mi Último Adiós (1896)

Type: Poem

Language: Spanish

English Title: My Last Farewell

Written on the eve of his execution, this poem is Rizal's farewell to his beloved country. It expresses his willingness to die for the Philippines and his hope for its freedom. The poem is considered one of the most moving and patriotic works in Philippine literature.

La Indolencia de los Filipinos (1890)

Type: Essay

Language: Spanish

English Title: The Indolence of the Filipinos

In this essay, Rizal refutes the Spanish claim that Filipinos were lazy by nature. He argues that colonial policies, oppression, lack of education, and economic stagnation were the real causes of perceived indolence among Filipinos.

Sobre la Nueva Ortografía de la Lengua Tagala (1890)

Type: Essay

Language: Spanish

English Title: On the New Orthography of the Tagalog Language

This linguistic essay proposes changes to Tagalog spelling to make it more consistent and phonetic. Rizal advocates for national identity through language reform.

Filipinas Dentro de Cien Años (1889–1890)

Type: Essay Series

Language: Spanish

English Title: The Philippines a Century Hence

A four-part essay predicting the future of the Philippines, Rizal examines the possible outcomes of continued Spanish rule, American intervention, or Philippine independence. It is a visionary work showcasing his analytical and prophetic insights.

A La Juventud Filipina (1879)

Type: Poem

Language: Spanish

English Title: To the Filipino Youth

Written when Rizal was only 18, this poem encourages the youth of the Philippines to cultivate their talents and take part in shaping the nation's future. It emphasizes education, progress, and national pride.

Junto Al Pasig (1880)

Type: Poem/Play

Language: Spanish

English Title: Beside the Pasig

A one-act play written for a school program, this allegorical drama features religious and patriotic themes, portraying the Philippines as a nation under the control of foreign powers and the Catholic Church.

José Rizal made significant contributions to the Philippines in literature, nationalism, education, science, and reform advocacy. Though he was not a revolutionary in the militant sense, his intellectual and moral leadership became the foundation of the Philippine revolution.

1. Nationalism and the Propaganda Movement

Rizal was a key figure in the Propaganda Movement, a group of Filipino reformists in Europe who sought changes in the Spanish colonial administration through peaceful means.

  • Promoted Filipino nationalism and unity.
  • Fought for equality between Filipinos and Spaniards under the law.
  • Advocated for freedom of speech, press, and assembly.
  • Helped awaken national consciousness among Filipinos.

2. Literary Contributions

Rizal used his writings to expose the ills of Spanish colonization.

  • Noli Me Tangere – exposed the corruption of the clergy and colonial government.
  • El Filibusterismo – showed the need for radical change through revolution.
  • Mi Último Adiós – a patriotic poem showing his love for the country even in death.

These Works:

  • Sparked the revolutionary movement.
  • Inspired leaders like Andres Bonifacio and Emilio Aguinaldo.

3. Education Advocacy

Rizal believed that education is key to liberation from oppression.

  • Advocated for the establishment of schools accessible to Filipinos.
  • Promoted education for both men and women (e.g., “Letter to the Young Women of Malolos”).
  • Opened a school for boys in Dapitan, teaching academic and practical skills.

4. Scientific and Medical Contributions

Rizal was trained as a doctor (ophthalmologist) and had deep interests in science and natural history.

  • Successfully performed eye surgeries (notably on his mother).
  • Collected flora and fauna specimens while in Dapitan (some species named after him).
  • Conducted geographic and ethnographic studies in Mindanao.

5. Linguistic and Cultural Contributions

  • Advocated for the preservation and development of Filipino languages.
  • Wrote essays like "Sobre la Nueva Ortografía de la Lengua Tagala".
  • Translated classic works into Tagalog to educate Filipinos.

6. Peaceful Reform Advocacy

Unlike other revolutionaries, Rizal sought peaceful reform rather than violent revolution.

  • Emphasized nonviolent resistance and moral transformation.
  • Believed change must come through enlightenment and civic virtue.

Though not a militant leader, his execution made him a martyr and a symbol of the Filipino struggle for freedom.

7. Life in Exile (Dapitan Contributions)

  • Lived in Dapitan (1892–1896) after being exiled.
  • Built a school, water system, and hospital.
  • Practiced medicine, taught students, and engaged in public works.
  • Lived a model life of service and simplicity that inspired future leaders.

8. Legacy

  • His life and writings inspired the 1896 Philippine Revolution.
  • Declared national hero by both the American colonial government and Filipino historians.
  • His ideas remain the foundation of modern Filipino identity and democratic values.

Monuments dedicated to Jose Rizal exist in various countries around the world. Some notable ones include:

Rizal Monument - Luneta Park, Manila, Philippines
The most famous monument marking the site of his execution and a national symbol.
Rizal Monument - Madrid, Spain
Honoring Rizal’s legacy in the former colonial power’s capital.
Rizal Monument - Wilhelmsfeld, Germany
Near the town where Rizal studied ophthalmology.
Rizal Memorial Statue - Chicago, Illinois, USA
Created by famed sculptor Richard Hunt, located in Lincoln Park.
Jose Rizal Park - Seattle, Washington, USA
Features a statue and commemorates Filipino-American heritage.
Rizal Monument - Cebu, Philippines
A prominent monument in Cebu City honoring the national hero.
Jose Rizal Statue - London, United Kingdom
A tribute statue in London reflecting Rizal’s global legacy.
Jose Rizal Statue - Berlin, Germany
Commemorative statue celebrating Rizal’s impact in Germany.
Jose Rizal Monument - Jakarta, Indonesia
A monument recognizing Rizal’s influence in Southeast Asia.

These monuments celebrate his enduring legacy as a national hero and reformer.
